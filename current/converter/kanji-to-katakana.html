Why Convert Kanji to Katakana?

Katakana (カタカナ) is the angular Japanese script used for foreign loanwords, emphasis, and — importantly — pronunciation fields on official paperwork. If you have ever filled in a Japanese form, you have probably met a small box labeled フリガナ above the name field. When that label is printed in katakana, the reading you write must be in katakana too. Banks, mobile carriers, and government offices routinely store name readings this way, so being able to turn kanji into katakana quickly is a genuinely practical skill.

Katakana is also the script of the dictionary: Japanese dictionaries conventionally list a kanji's on'yomi (音読み, the Sino-Japanese reading) in katakana and its kun'yomi (訓読み, the native reading) in hiragana. Seeing readings in katakana therefore mirrors exactly how you will encounter them when you look characters up.

東
On'yomi (katakana): トウ (tō)
Kun'yomi (hiragana): ひがし (higashi), あずま (azuma)
Examples: 東京 = トウキョウ (tōkyō, Tokyo) · 東口 = ヒガシグチ (higashiguchi, east exit)

Our converter uses morphological analysis — the same technology behind Japanese input methods and search engines — to split your text into words and select the correct context-dependent reading for each one, then presents every reading in katakana. Words already written in hiragana or katakana are carried through in katakana form, so the output line is uniformly katakana from start to finish.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I convert kanji to katakana?
Paste your Japanese text containing kanji into the input box and click Convert. The tool analyzes each word in context and displays its katakana reading, along with romaji and an English gloss. Switch views for a flat katakana line, romaji, or a full word-by-word table.
Why would I need kanji readings in katakana instead of hiragana?
Katakana readings are required on many Japanese forms — name fields labeled フリガナ expect katakana, and banks and official records often store name readings in katakana. Japanese dictionaries also list on'yomi (Sino-Japanese readings) in katakana, so katakana output is useful for studying kanji readings the way reference works present them.
What is the difference between フリガナ and ふりがな on Japanese forms?
Both ask for the pronunciation of your name, but the script of the label tells you which script to answer in: フリガナ written in katakana means write your reading in katakana, while ふりがな written in hiragana means write it in hiragana. This tool produces the katakana version; our kanji to hiragana converter produces the hiragana one.
Can this tool convert full sentences with mixed kanji, hiragana, and katakana?
Yes. The converter uses morphological analysis to segment the sentence into words, then outputs every word's reading in katakana. Existing katakana words are kept as they are, and hiragana words are promoted to katakana, so the whole output line reads uniformly in katakana.
Is the conversion accurate for Japanese names?
The tool chooses readings from context using dictionary-based analysis, which handles most common names correctly. Personal names can carry rare nanori readings, however, so always confirm the reading with the person or an official document before using it on paperwork.
Is this kanji to katakana converter free?
Yes — the tool is completely free, with no registration or limits. All conversion runs directly in your browser, so your text is never sent to a server.
