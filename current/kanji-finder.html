How the Handwritten Kanji Search Works

When you draw in the box, the finder compares your strokes against reference patterns for more than 2,200 kanji — every jōyō ("regular use") character and the most common beyond them. Matching happens in two passes: a coarse pass filters by stroke count and overall shape, then a fine pass scores stroke directions and positions to rank the best candidates. Because scoring is stroke-order tolerant, you don't need textbook-perfect calligraphy — a recognizable shape with roughly the right number of strokes is enough.

Unlike older handwritten kanji lookup sites that send every stroke to a server for processing, recognition here runs entirely in your browser. That makes it faster after the first load, it keeps working on a flaky connection, and your drawings never leave your device.

Find Kanji by Radical

Every kanji is assembled from smaller components — radicals (部首, bushu). If you can spot even one or two parts of an unknown character, the Radicals tab will find it: tap the components you recognize and the finder intersects the lists, narrowing thousands of kanji down to a handful in a couple of taps. Components that can no longer appear together with your selection are automatically greyed out, and a stroke-count filter narrows things further. The decomposition data covers 6,000+ kanji across 253 radicals.

Kanji Lookup by Reading or Meaning

Already know how a kanji is pronounced, or what it means? The Search tab looks up characters by English meaning ("water"), romaji ("mizu"), or kana (みず) — and you can paste a kanji directly to jump straight to its card. Results are ranked by how common each character is, so the kanji you're most likely looking for appears first.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I find a kanji by drawing it?
Draw the kanji in the box using your mouse, finger, or stylus. Candidates appear after every stroke and get more accurate as you add strokes. Click a candidate to see its readings, meanings, stroke order, and example words.
Do I need to use the correct stroke order?
No. The recognizer is stroke-order tolerant, so a roughly correct shape with approximately the right number of strokes is usually enough. Getting the stroke count close matters more than the order you draw them in — though the stroke-order animation on each kanji card can teach you the proper order too.
How does finding kanji by radical work?
Every kanji is built from smaller components called radicals. In the Radicals tab, tap the parts you can see in the character — the list instantly narrows to kanji containing all of them, and components that can no longer co-occur are greyed out. You can also filter by total stroke count.
Can I find a kanji from a picture or photo?
The most reliable way is to draw what you see in the picture into the drawing box — handwriting recognition then identifies it. This works even for stylized fonts, calligraphy, and tattoos, where automatic photo OCR often fails on decorative characters.
Is this better than drawing kanji in Google Translate?
Google Translate's handwriting input identifies a character but tells you almost nothing about it. This kanji finder shows readings, meanings, JLPT level, stroke-order animation, radicals, and example words for every result — and it runs entirely in your browser without sending your drawing to a server. If you need a whole sentence translated afterwards, our kanji converters take it from there.
What information does the kanji finder show for each character?
Each kanji card shows on'yomi readings in katakana, kun'yomi readings in hiragana, English meanings, stroke count, JLPT level, school grade, usage frequency, its radicals, an animated stroke-order diagram, and common example words with readings and translations.
Is the kanji finder free and private?
Yes. It is completely free with no registration, and everything — handwriting recognition included — runs locally in your browser. Your drawings and searches are never uploaded to a server.
