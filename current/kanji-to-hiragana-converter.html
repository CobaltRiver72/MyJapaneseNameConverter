How Japanese Kanji Reading Works

Kanji (漢字) are logographic characters originally borrowed from Chinese and adapted into the Japanese writing system over centuries. Modern Japanese uses approximately 2,136 jōyō kanji (常用漢字) — the "regular-use" characters designated by Japan's Ministry of Education. Each kanji can have multiple readings, which makes learning to read Japanese one of the most challenging aspects of the language.

Every kanji has at least two types of readings: Onyomi (音読み), the Sino-Japanese reading derived from the original Chinese pronunciation, and Kunyomi (訓読み), the native Japanese reading. The correct reading depends entirely on context — the words surrounding the kanji determine which pronunciation to use.

日
Onyomi: ニチ (nichi), ジツ (jitsu)
Kunyomi: ひ (hi), か (ka)
Examples: 日本 = にほん (nihon, Japan) · 日曜日 = にちようび (nichiyōbi, Sunday) · 今日 = きょう (kyō, today)

Our converter uses morphological analysis — the same technology used in Japanese word processors and search engines — to intelligently segment text and determine the correct context-dependent reading for each kanji. This means you get accurate hiragana readings even for kanji with many possible pronunciations.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I convert kanji to hiragana?
Simply paste your Japanese text containing kanji into the input box and click Convert. The tool will instantly display the hiragana reading for each kanji character. You can also choose different output modes like Furigana (readings above kanji), Romaji (romanized text), or Mixed (original with readings in parentheses).
What is the difference between hiragana and furigana?
Hiragana is one of the Japanese writing scripts used for native words, grammar particles, and more. Furigana are small hiragana characters placed above or beside kanji to indicate pronunciation — you'll commonly see them in manga, children's books, and Japanese learning materials. Our tool supports both inline hiragana conversion and furigana display modes.
Can this tool handle sentences with mixed kanji and hiragana?
Yes! The converter accurately processes mixed Japanese text. It uses morphological analysis to identify kanji characters and compound words, converting them while preserving existing hiragana, katakana, punctuation, and numbers exactly as they are.
Does the converter show multiple kanji readings?
The tool uses context-aware morphological analysis to determine the correct reading for each kanji based on the surrounding text. This means it automatically selects the right reading — for example, it knows that 日 is read as にち in 日曜日 but ひ in 日の出. This is the same technology used in Japanese word processors.
Is this tool free to use?
Yes, our kanji to hiragana converter is completely free with no registration required. All processing happens directly in your browser — your text is never sent to any server. Use it as many times as you need for your Japanese studies.
Why do some kanji have multiple readings?
Kanji were borrowed from Chinese over centuries, often multiple times from different Chinese dialects. Onyomi (音読み) reflects the original Chinese pronunciation, while Kunyomi (訓読み) is the native Japanese reading assigned to the character. Some kanji also have special readings called nanori (used in names) or jukujikun (compound-specific readings like 今日 = きょう).
Can I use this for JLPT study?
Absolutely! This tool is perfect for JLPT preparation at all levels (N5 through N1). Paste practice reading passages, news articles, or any Japanese text, and instantly see hiragana readings for unfamiliar kanji. The converter helps you learn readings in context, which is far more effective than memorizing isolated character lists.
